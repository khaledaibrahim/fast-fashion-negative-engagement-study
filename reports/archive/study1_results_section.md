# Study 1 Results Section

## Study 1 Results

Study 1 analysed low-rated fast-fashion reviews to explore how low service quality is expressed in naturally occurring consumer narratives and how these narratives connect to more severe negative consumer-brand responses. From the original review corpus, the exploratory sample was restricted to 1-3 star reviews, yielding 4,558 usable reviews across H&M, ZARA, SHEIN, and Urban Outfitters.

The filtered corpus was strongly negative overall, as expected from the sampling frame, but important differences emerged across brands. ZARA showed the most negative mean compound sentiment, while Urban Outfitters was comparatively less negative on average. The sentiment results should therefore be interpreted as indicators of intensity within an already negative complaint environment rather than as general brand sentiment.

Aspect coding showed that low service quality was concentrated in a recurring set of operational and relational failures. Delivery and logistics issues dominated for H&M, SHEIN, and Urban Outfitters, while customer service issues were especially prominent for ZARA. Refund and return problems were consistently salient across all four brands. Taken together, these patterns indicate that low service quality in fast fashion is experienced not only as fulfilment failure but also as an interactional and recovery failure.

Emotion coding further suggested that the complaint narratives involve more than simple dissatisfaction. Frustration was the most prevalent emotion marker across all brands, followed by disappointment, anger, and disgust. ZARA showed relatively stronger anger and disgust markers than the other brands, suggesting that some service-failure narratives escalate toward more aversive emotional responses.

The primary topic model for Study 1 was LDA. The six-topic solution produced interpretable complaint clusters that aligned well with the study’s conceptual focus. These topics were labelled as: In-Store Experience, Exchanges & Returns; Customer Service Failures & Staff Conduct; Product Quality, Size & Wrong Items; Refunds, Contact & Service Recovery; Order Delays, Shipping & Cancellation; and Delivery, Non-Receipt & Parcel Problems. Among these, the strongest coherence was observed for the order and delivery-oriented themes, indicating a particularly stable logistics-related complaint structure within the corpus. At the brand level, H&M was most strongly associated with order delays and shipping disruption, ZARA with in-store and customer-service failures, SHEIN with refunds and service recovery, and Urban Outfitters with delivery and parcel problems.

BERTopic was used as a complementary semantic robustness check. While its outlier class remained sizeable, it surfaced narrower issue clusters that added useful interpretive detail, including Shipping Delays & Online Fulfilment, Order Cancellation & Fulfilment Breakdown, Wrong Item & Wrong Size, Premium Delivery & Courier Failure, and Gift Card & Store Credit Issues. These more granular topics do not replace the clearer LDA structure, but they reinforce the conclusion that low service quality is experienced through a set of repeated, concrete failures rather than isolated incidents.

Taken together, the Study 1 findings support three substantive conclusions. First, low service quality in fast fashion is strongly rooted in delivery, service-recovery, payment, and frontline interaction failures. Second, many complaints reflect accumulation and repetition, providing exploratory support for negative past experience rather than one-off dissatisfaction alone. Third, some complaint clusters contain severe and hostile language that is more consistent with aversive brand rejection than with mild dissatisfaction, which provides a qualitative foundation for testing stronger downstream responses in Study 2.

Accordingly, Study 1 provides the exploratory basis for the confirmatory model tested in Study 2. The review narratives indicate that low service quality is the initiating condition, that repeated unresolved failures accumulate into negative past experience, and that some complaints escalate toward stronger aversive responses consistent with brand hate and eventual brand switching.
